Most people start in the wrong place.

They ask, "What is the coolest thing AI can do for me?" A better question is: **what repeated task in my week is expensive, annoying, and structured enough to improve?**

That is how you pick a first AI use case that teaches you something instead of burning time.

## What a good first use case looks like

Your first project should have four properties:

- it happens often
- it takes enough time to matter
- success is easy to judge
- mistakes are low-risk and fixable

Summarizing meetings, drafting first-pass emails, cleaning spreadsheet text, tagging inbound requests, or turning notes into action items are all strong starting points.

## What to avoid first

Avoid workflows that are high stakes, poorly defined, or politically sensitive.

That means no autonomous customer support, no legal review, no performance management decisions, and no "replace the team with agents" experiments. Those are later-stage systems, not first reps.

Also avoid one-off novelty projects. If a task only happens twice a year, even a great AI result will not compound.

## A selection test that works

Take three candidate tasks and score each one from 1 to 5 on:

- frequency
- time spent
- ease of review
- downside if wrong
- available source material

Then pick the task with the highest combined score, not the one that sounds most futuristic.

This is boring advice. It is also the advice that usually works.

## Start with assistance, not autonomy

The most reliable first setup is human-in-the-loop:

- AI drafts
- human reviews
- human decides

This teaches you where the model is useful, where it drifts, and what instructions improve output. You collect a real feedback loop before you automate anything further.

## Build the smallest useful workflow

Do not begin with a giant platform plan. Start with:

1. one task
2. one prompt or one tool
3. one review rubric
4. one simple success metric

For example:

- task: summarize weekly client calls
- rubric: accurate, under 150 words, include next steps and blockers
- metric: reduce summary writing time from 20 minutes to 5

That is enough to learn whether the workflow has legs.

## What to measure

Pick one or two metrics only:

- time saved
- error rate
- revision rate
- throughput

The key is to measure the workflow, not your excitement about the workflow.

## The useful takeaway

Your first AI use case should feel modest. That is a feature, not a bug.

Small, repeated, easy-to-review tasks are where people build actual skill. Once you can improve one workflow predictably, you have a base to expand from. Until then, bigger plans are usually just bigger ambiguity.
