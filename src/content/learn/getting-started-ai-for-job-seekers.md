Job searching is tedious. You're customizing resumes, writing cover letters, researching companies, preparing for interviews — and doing it dozens of times. AI handles the repetitive parts so you can focus on the human parts: networking, telling your story, and deciding what you actually want.

## Resume Tailoring

The single highest-ROI use of AI in job searching. Most applicants send the same resume to every job. Companies (and their ATS systems) want to see relevance.

### How to Do It

1. Paste the job description into ChatGPT, Claude, or your preferred AI tool
2. Paste your master resume
3. Ask: "Tailor my resume for this specific role. Emphasize relevant experience, use language from the job description where authentic, and suggest which items to move up or remove."

**Critical:** Review the output carefully. AI will sometimes exaggerate your experience or add skills you don't have. Your resume must be truthful — AI is helping you present your real experience in the most relevant way, not fabricating qualifications.

### What to Watch For

- **Keyword matching gone wrong** — AI might stuff in buzzwords that don't reflect your actual experience. Remove anything you can't discuss confidently in an interview.
- **Loss of personality** — AI-tailored resumes can sound generic. Keep your specific accomplishments and numbers. "Increased revenue by 32% through redesigned pricing strategy" beats "Drove revenue growth through strategic initiatives."
- **Over-optimization** — if your resume reads like a rephrased job description, it's too tailored. Recruiters notice.

## Cover Letters

AI is genuinely good at cover letter drafts. The template:

1. Provide the job description, your resume, and 2-3 specific reasons you're interested in this company
2. Ask for a cover letter that connects your experience to their needs and explains your genuine interest

Then rewrite the opening and closing in your own voice. The middle paragraphs (mapping your experience to their requirements) can stay closer to the AI draft. The beginning and end should sound like you.

**Pro tip:** Ask AI to write the cover letter in different tones — formal, conversational, enthusiastic — and pick the one closest to how you naturally communicate. Then edit from there.

## Company Research

Before an interview, ask AI:

- "Summarize [Company]'s recent news, product launches, and strategic direction"
- "What are the biggest challenges facing [Industry] right now?"
- "Based on this job description, what problems is this team likely trying to solve?"

Then verify with primary sources. AI gives you a research starting point in minutes instead of hours. Always check recent news directly — AI's training data has a cutoff, and you want current information.

## Interview Preparation

### Behavioral Questions

Give AI the job description and your background. Ask: "Generate 10 likely behavioral interview questions for this role and help me draft STAR-format answers based on my experience."

Practice speaking the answers out loud. Written answers and spoken answers sound different. The AI draft gives you the content; you need to make it sound natural.

### Technical Preparation

For technical roles, AI can:
- Generate practice problems appropriate to the role's level
- Explain concepts you're rusty on
- Walk through system design questions step by step
- Review your solutions and point out edge cases

### Questions to Ask

"Based on this job description and what I know about the company, suggest 5 thoughtful questions I could ask the interviewer." AI generates better questions than most candidates prepare on their own because it considers the full context.

## LinkedIn and Networking

AI helps with:
- **Profile optimization** — paste your profile and target role, ask for improvement suggestions
- **Connection messages** — draft personalized outreach that references specific shared interests or experiences (never send generic AI messages)
- **Post writing** — share industry insights or career reflections to build visibility

The rule for networking messages: shorter is better, specific is better, genuine is better. If your message could be sent to anyone, it won't work.

## What AI Can't Do

- **Replace genuine connections.** The job search still runs on relationships. AI helps you prepare and communicate, but a warm introduction from someone who knows your work beats any AI-optimized application.
- **Guarantee accuracy.** AI will confidently suggest strategies that don't apply to your specific field or level. Cross-reference AI advice with industry-specific resources.
- **Read the room.** Some companies value formal applications; others prefer casual. Some interviewers want structured answers; others want conversation. AI gives you material; you read the situation.
- **Be you.** If you get hired based on an AI-generated persona, you'll struggle to maintain it. Use AI to present yourself clearly, not to present someone else.

## The Ethical Line

There's a spectrum from "using AI to organize your thoughts" to "having AI do your work." For job searching:

✅ **Fine:** Using AI to tailor your resume, draft cover letters you edit, prepare for interviews, research companies
⚠️ **Careful:** Using AI to complete take-home assignments (check their AI policy), writing LinkedIn posts entirely with AI
❌ **Don't:** Claiming AI-generated work as solely yours in work samples, using AI during live assessments where it's prohibited, misrepresenting your skills

Most employers expect candidates to use AI tools — they use them too. The goal is demonstrating your judgment, communication, and thinking, with AI as an efficiency tool.

## Getting Started Today

1. Create a master resume with all your experience, skills, and accomplishments
2. Pick 3 job postings you're interested in
3. Use AI to tailor your resume for each one
4. Review critically — does each tailored version still sound like you? Is everything truthful?
5. Use the tailored version to draft a cover letter
6. Research each company with AI as your starting point

Time investment: about 30 minutes per application instead of 2+ hours. Quality: higher, because you're spending your time on judgment calls instead of formatting and rewording.
