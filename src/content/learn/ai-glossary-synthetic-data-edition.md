Synthetic data has gone from research curiosity to core infrastructure for training modern AI systems. This glossary covers the essential terminology, defined in plain English.

## Core Concepts

**Synthetic data.** Data generated by algorithms or models rather than collected from real-world observations. Used to augment training datasets, protect privacy, or create data for scenarios that are rare or expensive to observe in practice.

**Data augmentation.** Transforming existing real data to create new training examples. Classic examples: rotating images, adding noise to audio, paraphrasing text. The augmented data is derived from real data, not generated from scratch.

**Generative augmentation.** Using generative models (GANs, diffusion models, LLMs) to create new training examples rather than applying rule-based transformations. Produces more diverse and realistic augmentations but requires careful quality control.

**Seed data.** The initial set of real or hand-crafted examples used to bootstrap a synthetic data generation pipeline. Seed data defines the target distribution, style, and quality bar. Poor seed data leads to poor synthetic data at scale.

**Distribution matching.** The goal of ensuring synthetic data follows the same statistical distribution as the real data it's meant to represent. Measured through metrics like maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), Frechet inception distance (FID), or domain-specific statistical tests.

**Fidelity.** How closely synthetic data resembles real data at the individual sample level. High fidelity means individual synthetic examples are hard to distinguish from real ones. Not always necessary -- sometimes diversity matters more than fidelity.

**Diversity.** The degree to which synthetic data covers the range of variation present in the real data distribution. A generator that produces highly realistic but repetitive outputs has high fidelity but low diversity.

## Privacy and Safety

**Differential privacy.** A mathematical framework guaranteeing that the output of a computation doesn't reveal whether any specific individual's data was included in the input. Applied to synthetic data generation to ensure generated samples don't leak information about real individuals in the training data.

**Privacy budget (epsilon).** The parameter in differential privacy that controls the privacy-utility trade-off. Lower epsilon means stronger privacy but noisier (less useful) synthetic data. Typical values range from 1 to 10, though acceptable values depend on the sensitivity of the data.

**Membership inference.** An attack that determines whether a specific data point was part of a model's training data. Synthetic data generators must be tested against membership inference attacks to verify they don't memorize and reproduce real training examples.

**Memorization.** When a generative model reproduces training examples verbatim or near-verbatim in its output. A critical failure mode for synthetic data generators, especially when the training data contains sensitive information. Deduplication and privacy testing help detect it.

**Data contamination.** When data from a test or evaluation set leaks into training data, making evaluation metrics unreliable. In the synthetic data context, this happens when a generator trained on data that includes benchmark examples produces synthetic data containing those benchmarks.

**Validation set contamination.** A specific form of contamination where synthetic training data overlaps with validation or test data, either because the generator memorized test examples or because the same source was used for both. Undermines the integrity of model evaluation.

## Generation Methods

**Self-instruct.** A technique where an LLM generates its own instruction-following training data. The model produces instructions, inputs, and outputs, which are filtered and used to fine-tune itself or another model. Introduced by Wang et al. (2022) and foundational to many open-source instruction-tuned models.

**Evol-instruct.** An extension of self-instruct where existing instructions are iteratively made more complex by an LLM. Produces harder and more diverse training examples by "evolving" simple instructions into multi-step, nuanced ones.

**Constitutional AI data.** Training data generated through a process where an AI critiques and revises its own outputs according to a set of principles (a "constitution"). Used to train models that are helpful, harmless, and honest without relying entirely on human feedback.

**RLHF data (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback).** Preference data where humans compare model outputs and indicate which is better. Used to train reward models that guide policy optimization. Expensive to collect, leading to synthetic alternatives.

**DPO data (Direct Preference Optimization).** Preference pairs (chosen and rejected responses) used to train models directly on human preferences without a separate reward model. Can be synthetically generated by having a strong model judge pairs of outputs from a weaker model.

**Distillation data.** Training data generated by a larger, more capable "teacher" model for training a smaller "student" model. The student learns to replicate the teacher's behavior from the teacher's outputs. A common and effective form of synthetic data generation.

## Quality and Filtering

**Deduplication.** Removing duplicate or near-duplicate examples from a dataset. Critical for synthetic data because generators can produce repetitive outputs. Methods range from exact string matching to embedding-based similarity thresholds.

**Quality scoring.** Assigning a quality score to each synthetic example, typically using a separate model or heuristic. Low-scoring examples are filtered out. Common approaches: perplexity-based scoring, reward model scoring, and execution-based verification (for code and math).

**Execution-verified generation.** A quality assurance method for code and math synthetic data where generated solutions are executed against test cases. Only examples that produce correct outputs are kept. Produces significantly higher quality data than text-only filtering.

**Perplexity filtering.** Using a language model's perplexity score (how "surprised" it is by a text) to filter synthetic data. Very low perplexity may indicate trivial or repetitive examples. Very high perplexity may indicate incoherent text. A band-pass filter in perplexity space often works well.

## Failure Modes

**Model collapse.** A degenerative process where a model trained on synthetic data from a previous generation of models progressively loses diversity and quality. Each generation's synthetic data is slightly less diverse, and errors compound. After several generations without real-data anchoring, the model produces increasingly narrow, repetitive output.

**Mode collapse.** When a generator produces data concentrated around a few modes of the real distribution, ignoring the full range of variation. The output looks realistic but lacks diversity. Common in GAN-based generation.

**Amplification of bias.** Synthetic data generators can amplify biases present in their training data. If the seed data over-represents certain demographics or perspectives, the synthetic data may exaggerate those imbalances. Bias auditing of synthetic datasets is essential.

## Data Mixing

**Real-synthetic mixing ratio.** The proportion of real to synthetic data in a training dataset. Research consistently finds that a mix outperforms pure synthetic or pure real data. Optimal ratios vary by task, but 60-70% real and 30-40% synthetic is a common starting range for fine-tuning.

**Curriculum mixing.** Strategically varying the real-synthetic ratio during training. One approach: start with more synthetic data (broad coverage) and increase the proportion of real data toward the end of training (refinement). Analogous to curriculum learning.

**Data flywheel.** A self-reinforcing cycle where a deployed model generates data (through user interactions or synthetic generation) that is used to improve the next version of the model. When managed well, each iteration produces a better model with better data. When managed poorly, it leads to model collapse.
