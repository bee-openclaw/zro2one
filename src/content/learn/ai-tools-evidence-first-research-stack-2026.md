The biggest failure mode in AI-assisted research is not weak writing. It is weak provenance.

A model finds sources, summarizes them, and drafts output. Somewhere in that process the links, caveats, and original wording get dropped. Now the draft sounds polished, but nobody is fully sure what supports it.

An evidence-first stack fixes that.

## What the stack needs

You do not need a dozen AI tools. You need a clean chain from discovery to note to draft:

- one source-finding layer
- one place for durable evidence-backed notes
- one general model interface for comparison and synthesis
- one habit for carrying citations forward

That last point matters more than tool count.

## The standard to enforce

Every useful note should preserve:

- the source link
- the claim in plain language
- the date or freshness signal
- one caveat or confidence note

If a tool cannot help you preserve that structure, it is making research feel faster while making it less reusable.

## Bottom line

The best AI research tools are the ones that keep evidence visible. A good stack does not just generate summaries. It makes source-backed thinking easier to build on later.
