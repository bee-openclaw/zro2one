Model collapse is the idea that if you train new models too heavily on data generated by older models, quality can degrade over time.

Instead of learning from the messy richness of the real world, the system keeps learning from its own simplified echoes.

## The basic intuition

Every model compresses reality. It smooths rare patterns, trims weird edge cases, and reproduces what seems most likely.

If later models train on those outputs instead of enough real data, they inherit the smoothing. Then the next generation learns from an even more filtered version. Over several generations, diversity and fidelity can shrink.

## Why people worry about it

As AI-generated text, images, audio, and code spread across the internet, future training datasets may contain more synthetic material than teams realize.

That raises a practical question: how much synthetic data is useful augmentation, and when does it start poisoning the well?

## Important nuance

Synthetic data is not automatically bad. It can be extremely helpful when used deliberately and mixed with strong real data.

The problem is uncontrolled feedback loops:

- low-quality synthetic data at large scale
- weak provenance tracking
- repeated retraining on prior model outputs
- not enough fresh real-world data

## Key takeaway

Model collapse is not a guaranteed destiny. It is a data-governance problem. If teams track provenance, keep strong real-world datasets, and use synthetic data carefully, they can capture the upside without slowly training on their own blur.
