Executive assistants already do the kind of work AI is unusually good at supporting: summarizing information, turning rough notes into polished drafts, organizing follow-up, and handling repetitive communication tasks.

The opportunity is real. So is the risk, because assistants often touch the most sensitive information in an organization.

## High-value starting points

### Drafting routine communication

AI is useful for first drafts of meeting agendas, travel notes, event reminders, and internal follow-up messages.

### Brief preparation

Feed in background notes, recent emails, and prior decisions, then ask AI for a concise prep brief before a meeting.

### Meeting follow-up

Turn notes into action items, owner lists, and clean summaries. This is one of the fastest wins.

### Information synthesis

Assistants are often asked to combine inputs from several people into one clear update. AI is excellent at first-pass synthesis when the source material is already trusted.

## Rules that matter

- do not paste sensitive information into tools you have not approved
- verify names, dates, and commitments before sending
- treat AI as a draft partner, not an authorized sender
- keep a human checkpoint on anything externally visible

## A simple first-week plan

1. Use AI for agenda drafting.
2. Use it for meeting recap cleanup.
3. Use it to turn messy notes into polished bullet points.
4. Skip anything involving confidential strategy until you know the tool boundaries.

## Key takeaway

For executive assistants, AI is most valuable when it reduces administrative drag without touching judgment, discretion, or trust. The win is not "automate the role." The win is reclaiming time for the parts of the role only a capable human can do.
